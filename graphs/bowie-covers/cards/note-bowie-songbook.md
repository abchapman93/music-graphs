One of the things that distinguishes [[person:david-bowie]] as a songwriter is the unusually wide range of artists who have made his songs their own. Most of the great rock songwriters get covered, but Bowie's catalog has crossed into idioms that rarely overlap: into Brazilian acoustic guitar music via [[person:seu-jorge]]'s recordings of [[song:rebel-rebel]] and other Hunky Dory–era songs; into modern jazz piano-trio music via [[group:bad-plus]]'s instrumental treatment of [[song:life-on-mars]]; and into grunge via [[group:nirvana]]'s acoustic [[song:the-man-who-sold-the-world]]. The breadth speaks to two things: how strongly Bowie's melodies stand on their own, decoupled from the specific arrangement and persona of the original, and how deeply Bowie himself listened across genres — borrowing soul, electronic music, German art rock, and 1970s glam in ways that left openings for the cover artists who came after him.
