Stanley Turrentine was an American tenor saxophonist born in [[location:pittsburgh]], known for one of the most soulful, instantly recognizable tones in jazz. Raised in a musical Hill District family — his older brother was the trumpeter Tommy Turrentine — he came up playing rhythm and blues with Lowell Fulson and Earl Bostic before joining Max Roach's group in the late 1950s. Through the 1960s he was a fixture on Blue Note Records, recording a long series of hard bop and soul-jazz dates as a leader and as a sideman, often with organist Jimmy Smith and with his then-wife, organist Shirley Scott. He moved toward a more commercial soul-jazz and crossover sound on CTI Records in the 1970s, beginning with the breakthrough album [[album:sugar]] in 1970. Its title track, [[track:sugar-track]], became one of the most-played jazz tunes of the era. Turrentine continued recording and touring through the 1990s, never losing the bluesy, vocalized quality that defined his playing.
