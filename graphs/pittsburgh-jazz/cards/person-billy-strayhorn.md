Billy Strayhorn was an American jazz composer, pianist, lyricist, and arranger, best known as Duke Ellington's closest musical collaborator. Though born in Dayton, Ohio, he grew up in [[location:pittsburgh]] and considered it his hometown; he studied at Pittsburgh Musical Institute and absorbed the city's rich jazz scene alongside contemporaries like [[person:errol-garner]]. In 1938 he met Ellington in Pittsburgh and was soon writing for the Ellington Orchestra. Their partnership lasted nearly three decades and produced a vast body of work in which the line between composer and arranger is often impossible to draw. Strayhorn wrote "Take the 'A' Train," which became Ellington's signature theme, along with "Lush Life," "Chelsea Bridge," and "Daydream." Openly gay at a time when that carried enormous risk, he lived with quiet dignity in New York's interracial bohemian circles. Ellington's tribute album *And His Mother Called Him Bill*, recorded after Strayhorn's death from cancer in 1967, remains one of the most moving statements of musical friendship in jazz.
