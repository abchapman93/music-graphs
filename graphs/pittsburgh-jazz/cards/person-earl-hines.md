Earl "Fatha" Hines was an American jazz pianist and bandleader, one of the most influential pianists in the history of the music. Born just outside [[location:pittsburgh]] in Duquesne, Pennsylvania, he moved into the city as a teenager and worked the Pittsburgh club scene before relocating to Chicago in the 1920s. There he forged a famous partnership with Louis Armstrong, recording the celebrated 1928 sides that helped define modern jazz piano. His "trumpet-style" right-hand approach — playing single-note lines in octaves against a strong, swinging left hand — broke pianists out of stride and ragtime conventions and pointed the way toward bebop. As a bandleader at the Grand Terrace Cafe in Chicago through the 1930s, he employed early bop figures including Charlie Parker and Dizzy Gillespie. Hines continued performing into the 1970s, by which point he was widely recognized as a living link between early jazz and its modern forms. His Pittsburgh roots tie him directly to the Hill District tradition that also produced [[person:billy-strayhorn]] and [[person:erroll-garner]].
